# LinkedIn

## How the Algorithm Works

LinkedIn ranks content through a three-stage pipeline: spam filtering, initial audience testing (first 60 minutes), and network expansion based on engagement signals and topic relevance.

**What matters most:**

1. **Time on post** — How long readers spend with your content. Carousels and long-form text win here because they take time to consume.
2. **Niche consistency** — Posting about the same topic area repeatedly builds algorithmic trust. Jumping between topics hurts distribution.
3. **Genuine interaction** — The algorithm detects fake engagement patterns (comment pods, coordinated likes). Authentic replies from real connections carry the most weight.
4. **Relevance persistence** — Posts can resurface 2-3 weeks later if they're relevant to someone's interests. LinkedIn isn't just a feed — it's a recommendation engine.
5. **No outbound links** — Posts with external URLs get significantly less reach. Keep value inside the platform.

**The first hour matters.** Initial engagement in the first 60 minutes determines whether your post reaches second and third-degree connections. Post when your audience is active, not when you happen to finish writing.

**Current landscape:** Organic reach has tightened considerably. Quality and specificity beat volume. Post 2-3 times per week, not daily.

## Formats

| Format | Dimensions | Sweet Spot |
|--------|-----------|------------|
| Carousel (PDF) | 1080x1350px (4:5) | 5-10 slides — highest engagement, most saves |
| Text post | 1,200-1,500 characters | 3-line hook, structured body, single CTA |
| Document | PDF upload, 10-15 pages | Good for detailed frameworks |
| Native video | 30-90 seconds, captioned | Lower reach than text/carousels but personal |

Carousels are the dominant format. They force longer dwell time (each swipe = more time on post), they're save-worthy, and they can be adapted for Instagram with minor tweaks.

## Carousel Structure

| Slide | Job |
|-------|-----|
| 1 | Stop the scroll — bold text, high contrast, one idea, creates curiosity |
| 2 | Deepen the hook — "here's the problem" or "what most people get wrong" |
| 3-7 | One point per slide — large text, minimal words, clear hierarchy |
| 8 | Recap — all points summarized for quick reference |
| 9 | Call to action — follow, repost, save |

**Design specifications:**
- **Dimensions:** 1080x1350px (4:5 ratio) — takes up maximum feed real estate on mobile
- **Headlines:** 40-60pt bold, one line if possible, two max
- **Body text:** 24-32pt, high readability — if someone can't read it on a phone without zooming, it's too small
- **Colors:** High contrast required. Dark text on light background or light text on dark. Avoid mid-tones that wash out on screens. If style guide exists, use brand palette — if not, stick to black/white/one accent color
- **Fonts:** Sans-serif for headlines, sans-serif or clean serif for body. Never more than 2 fonts per carousel. If style guide specifies fonts, use those
- **Spacing:** Generous whitespace around text blocks. Let the slide breathe. Cramped slides get swiped past
- **Visual consistency:** Every slide should look like it belongs to the same set — same font pairing, same color scheme, same layout grid. Don't redesign slide-to-slide
- **Logo/handle:** Bottom corner of last slide only. Not on every slide — it's noise
- **File format:** Export as PDF for native LinkedIn carousel upload. PNG sequence works but PDF is cleaner

## Opening Patterns

These are starting points. After a few runs with feedback, your own winning patterns should replace these.

**1. Evidence Opener**
> "I [tracked/measured/tested] [specific thing] for [time period]. The results surprised me."

**2. Reversal**
> "The advice everyone gives about [topic] is backwards. I did the opposite. Here's what happened."

**3. Numbered Promise**
> "I've distilled [impressive experience] into [N] lessons. Number [N] changed how I work."

**4. Tension Builder**
> "There's a reason [common frustration] keeps happening — and it's not what LinkedIn tells you."

**5. Direct Challenge**
> "[Widely accepted practice] is wasting your time. I stopped doing it six months ago. Here's the data."

**6. Specific Result**
> "[Concrete outcome] from [simple method]. No [common excuse]. Here's the full breakdown."

**7. Before/After Contrast**
> "A year ago I was [struggling state]. Today I [improved state]. The shift wasn't what I expected."

**8. Confession**
> "I need to be honest about something I got completely wrong about [topic]."

**9. Micro-Story**
> "A client asked me [specific question] last week. My answer surprised both of us."

**10. Pattern Recognition**
> "I've worked with [N] [type of client/company]. The ones who [succeed/fail] all have one thing in common."

## What Not to Do on LinkedIn

- **Don't post motivational fluff.** "Rise and grind" content gets engagement from the wrong audience. Specificity beats inspiration.
- **Don't link out in the post body.** Put links in comments if you must share them. Or better — keep the value inside the post.
- **Don't post daily.** The algorithm rewards quality signals, not volume. Three strong posts beat seven filler posts.
- **Don't use hashtags in the body text.** They break reading flow. Put them at the very end, after a separator, 3-5 max.
- **Don't write hooks that don't pay off.** "I was fired at 25. Best thing that happened." only works if the actual lesson is worth the setup.
- **Don't ignore comments.** Replying to comments in the first hour extends your post's algorithmic life. Treat comments as part of the content.
